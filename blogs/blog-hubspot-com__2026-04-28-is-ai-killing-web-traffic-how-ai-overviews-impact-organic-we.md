We’re in the middle of a huge shift in how search engines surface information, and it’s rewriting the rules for marketers and content teams across every industry.</p> 
<p>First, <a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/ai-overviews">Google’s AI Overviews</a> are answering queries directly on the results page, intercepting searches that previously drove clicks to websites. And second, a growing portion of searchers are skipping Google entirely and turning to answer engines like ChatGPT and Perplexity for answers.</p> 
<p>Both <a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/evolution-of-search">trends slice the traffic search engines</a> send to sites, but it’s not gone entirely. I’ve spent the last year navigating the ebbs and flows of traffic with HubSpot, and we’re learning how to balance AI behavior and website traffic expectations. Here’s what businessess need to know.</p>

<h2>Is AI killing web traffic?</h2> 
<p>AI Overviews change how users interact with search results by reducing CTR for some informational queries and redistributing clicks rather than eliminating all website traffic. Simple fact-based queries are more likely to trigger zero-click results, while more detailed, branded questions like comparisons are more likely to earn clicks when users need depth and validation.</p> 
<p>Marketers and brands that invest in AEO to help capture AI overviews rather than ignoring them are the brands that will stay competitive. <h2>What AI Overviews Change on the SERP</h2> 
<p>AI Overviews are generated summaries that appear at the top of Google’s search results, <em>above</em> both paid ads and organic listings. When one appears for a brand’s target query, it answers the user’s question, pushing all of the blue links farther down the page.</p> 
<p>And marketers all know what happens the farther down they appear on a SERP.</p> 
<p>If a brand is the site cited in the overview, impressions stay up (or grow), but clicks drop, and even if its website ranks well, clicks drop because users likely already got their answer in the overview.</p> 
<p>In the example below, “What is Bollywood?” notice how even big names like Masterclass and popular mediums like YouTube video can be pushed multiple scrolls below the fold.</p>

<h2>How to Measure AI Overviews’ Impact on Your Traffic</h2> 
<p>The problem of how to measure AI Overviews’ impact on web traffic is real. <a href="https://search.google.com/search-console/about">Google Search Console</a> currently does not offer a direct way to isolate or filter data for AI Overviews (AIO).</p> 
<p>All performance metrics from AI Overviews are aggregated with standard web search data. For instance, when a brand’s content is cited in an AI Overview, Search Console doesn’t tell them. Their impressions and clicks are logged, but merged with everything else.</p>

<h3>What is the best way to forecast traffic under AI Overviews?</h3> 
<p>I spoke with Amanda Sellers, HubSpot’s blog growth manager, about the best ways to forecast traffic under AI overviews.</p> 
<p>She recommends using linear regression, a mathematical method that uses past data to simulate a trend into the future. A linear regression assumes that nothing big — like an algorithm update or increase in SERP features like AI Overviews — will disrupt that trend.</p> 
<p>“You and I both know that Google likes to throw a wrench into things,” explains Sellers.</p> 
<p>“At one point, AI Overviews showed up for less than 10% of the HubSpot blog’s keywords, most of them being informational definition intent. Today, nearly 50% of the keywords the HubSpot blog ranks for have an AI Overview at the top.”</p> 
<p>For this reason, Sellers frequently checks AI Overview exposure in Ahrefs and performs CTR curve analysis using data from Google Search Console. That way, multiple scenarios can be forecasted on top of the baseline linear regression, such as “what if AI Overviews increase by 20%” or “what if we get impacted negatively by an algorithm update.”</p> 
<h3>How do you attribute changes to AI Overviews vs seasonality?</h3> 
<p>Linear regressions also allow marketers to quantify seasonal changes, determining patterns in historical data.</p> 
<p>For example, there might be a historical pattern of low traffic in December compared to November due to holiday seasonality. A linear regression can help marketers and SEO strategists create seasonality modifiers that adjust the traffic baseline according to the average pattern.</p> 
<p>Sellers continues, “If we take the baseline traffic, December usually lands 65% below the baseline because fewer people are searching. January tends to be one of our stronger months at around 135% above the baseline. Adding these fluctuations into our model can help us understand if there is unexpected performance in one direction or another.”</p> 
<p><a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/early-q4-business-data">If a traffic forecast already factored in seasonality</a> in this way, any performance anomalies in one way or another would mean seasonality is not the culprit. From there, an SEO strategist can use Ahrefs to determine whether Google increased the visibility of AIOs or whether another factor was at play. However, it’s not always that simple.</p> 
<p>“Keywords rise and fall, AIOs appear and disappear, algorithm updates come and go… and there are internal technical factors that can impact performance. In reality, attributing performance is so much more complex.”</p> 
<p>For instance, after a particularly tough algorithm update, Sellers found 46.7% of a subsection of HubSpot’s keywords lost positioning <em>and </em>gained an AI Overview. It’s much more difficult to attribute how much of the performance change was the AI Overview siphoning traffic versus a decrease in CTR from simply a lower SERP position.</p> 
<p>For this reason, it’s best to let the data speak for itself. Sellers split the keywords into different buckets:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li>Position Decreased AND AIO Present.</li> 
 <li>Position Decreased NO AIO Present.</li> 
 <li>Position Gain/Flat AND AIO Present.</li> 
 <li>Position Gain/Flat NO AIO Present.</li> 
</ul> 
<p>By comparing the performance of these buckets against each other and swapping CTRs, Sellers was able to get an estimate of how much performance change came from positioning changes versus AIOs.</p> 
<p>(Spoiler alert: AIOs were the much bigger culprit.)</p> 
<p>By comparing, Sellers found that even keywords where HubSpot didn’t lose positioning still had significant CTR losses. This means there was less traffic, even when we were performing well. Meanwhile, by swapping CTAs and multiplying by impressions, we could estimate the traffic decline.</p>

<h2>Is AI killing web traffic more for certain queries?</h2> 
<p>Not all queries are affected by AI Overviews. Thankfully, the data is becoming clearer about which types feel the greatest zero-click impact and which can still drive website traffic for businesses.</p> 
<h3>Queries Most Vulnerable to Zero-Click</h3> 
<p>In 2025, <a href="https://www.semrush.com/blog/ai-overviews-study/?hubs_content%3Dblog.hubspot.com/marketing/author/ramona-sukhraj%26hubs_content-cta%3Dai-and-seo-what-ai-means-for-the-future-of-seo-expert-tips-interview">Semrush reported that nearly 95%</a> of keywords triggering AI Overviews have little to no paid ads or commercial value. In other words, Google seems to be deploying AI summaries mainly for informational searches, with transactional content (i.e., pricing pages, demo pages) staying in the traditional SERP format.</p> 
<p>That means the website traffic most at risk is top-of-funnel educational content that typically grabs a lot of clicks for businesses and builds brand awareness.</p> 
<p>Think simple right-or-wrong lookups (“what is [concept],” “how to” explainers, definition queries, and single-source informational questions), like this example: “Who is Shahrukh Khan?”</p> 
<p><img alt="is killing website traffic; ai overviews being tracked in ahrefs" height="607" src="https://53.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hub/53/hubfs/is%20killing%20website%20traffic%3B%20ai%20overviews%20being%20tracked%20in%20ahrefs.webp?width=650&amp;height=607&amp;name=is%20killing%20website%20traffic%3B%20ai%20overviews%20being%20tracked%20in%20ahrefs.webp" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block; width: 650px; height: auto;" width="650" /></p> 
<p>This question is answered by Google in an AI overview so there’s less need to continue on to the other results.</p> 
<h3>Queries That Still Earn the Click</h3> 
<p>The same study found that transactional keywords like “buy,” “compare,” and “near me” tend to have higher CTRs because AI typically doesn’t complete transactions. Continuing our example, look at the results of “Buy Shahrukh Khan DVD.” (A DVD, for my younger folks, is a “digital video disc,” what we used to watch movies before streaming.)</p> 
<p><img alt="is killing website traffic; who is shahrukh khan answered in ai overview" height="271" src="https://53.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hub/53/hubfs/is%20killing%20website%20traffic%3B%20who%20is%20shahrukh%20khan%20answered%20in%20ai%20overview.webp?width=650&amp;height=271&amp;name=is%20killing%20website%20traffic%3B%20who%20is%20shahrukh%20khan%20answered%20in%20ai%20overview.webp" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block; width: 650px; height: auto;" width="650" /></p> 
<p>Comparison queries like “X vs. Y for [use case]” also continue to drive clicks, because users want depth and validation that a two-paragraph AI summary can’t fully provide. The same is true for queries that require local, real-time, or highly specific information.</p> 
<p>Overall, the best content for generating clicks and website traffic is currently bottom-funnel content (pricing pages, comparison guides, case studies), local service queries, niche technical queries, and original research that AI can’t synthesize from elsewhere.</p>

<h2>Is AI killing web traffic, or do you get traffic from AI citations?</h2> 
<p>Ok, so here’s where the picture shifts from bleak to nuanced: Being cited in an AI Overview may slash a brand’s top-of-the-funnel, awareness website traffic, but those who <em>do</em> visit are arguably more qualified.</p> 
<p><a href="https://www.dataslayer.ai/blog/google-ai-overviews-the-end-of-traditional-ctr-and-how-to-adapt-in-2025">A study from Dataslayer</a> found that brands cited in AI Overviews earn 35% more organic clicks and 91% more paid clicks than brands not cited in the same queries. Whether this is due to greater awareness or other factors is hard to say, but it’s still encouraging.</p> 
<p>Sure, a brand can’t control whether an AI Overview appears for their target query, but they can work to earn the citation when it does.</p> 
<h3>Optimizing for AI Overviews</h3> 
<p>To improve a brand’s chances of securing AI overviews, marketers need to learn <a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/how-to-write-for-ai-search">how to write for AI search</a> and invest in <a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/answer-engine-optimization-best-practices">answer engine optimization</a> (AEO). Here’s what that entails:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li><strong>Write in clear semantic blocks.</strong> Structure content in 200–400-word sections with explicit headings, summary boxes, and logical Q&amp;A formatting. AI systems use retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and favor content that’s chunked and scannable in this way.</li> 
 <li><strong>Lead with the answer. </strong>AI doesn’t read entire articles. Instead, it identifies answer-like structures (short paragraphs after questions, numbered steps, comparison tables). So, lead every key section with a 40–60-word direct answer that fully addresses the question, similar to <a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/how-to-featured-snippet-box?hubs_content%3Dblog.hubspot.com/marketing/answer-engine-optimization-best-practices%26hubs_content-cta%3Dhow-you-would-typically-go-after-featured-snippets">how marketers would typically go after “featured snippets”</a> in Google.</li> 
 <li><strong>Use structured data.</strong> <a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/schema-markup?hubs_content%3Dblog.hubspot.com/marketing/answer-engine-optimization-best-practices%26hubs_content-cta%3Dschema-markup">Schema markup</a> (FAQ, HowTo, Article) improves machine readability and increases the likelihood that content is parsed and surfaced.</li> 
 <li><strong>Cite primary sources inline.</strong> Verifiable, dated claims with source links are the hallmark of citable content. Vague assertions don’t get picked up.</li> 
 <li><strong>Publish and refresh frequently.</strong> Fresh content outperforms stale content in AI citations — update timestamps and material regularly to signal recency.</li> 
 <li><strong>Build topical authority.</strong> AI wants to know that it’s citing trustworthy, reliable experts to users. So, make sure to establish proof of a company or author’s expertise extensively in their online presence. That means both sharing expert knowledge through content on and off an owned site, but also getting quoted and cited by others, having good product reviews, etc.</li> 
</ul>

<h3><strong>Optimizing for Answer Engines (AEO)</strong></h3> 
<p>Even Google aside, a growing share of users are starting their search journey with AI through ChatGPT, Perplexity, or other answer engines.</p> 
<p>BrightLocal research shows that Google still drives <a href="https://www.brightlocal.com/research/consumer-search-behavior/%2523section-1-google-still-dominating-general-search?hubs_content%3Dblog.hubspot.com/marketing/author/ramona-sukhraj%26hubs_content-cta%3D8-generative-engine-optimization-best-practices-your-strategy-needs">61% of all general searches</a>, but more importantly, AI referral traffic tends to <a href="https://www.position.digital/blog/ai-seo-statistics/">convert at a dramatically higher rate</a>.</p> 
<p>To earn that high-intent traffic, teams need <a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/generative-engine-optimization-best-practices">Answer Engine Optimization (AEO)</a>:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li><strong>Create citation-ready content.</strong> Structured, authoritative content with specific, verifiable claims is what answer engines pull from. Data-heavy articles and definitive guides consistently outperform opinion pieces.</li> 
 <li><strong>Build cross-platform presence.</strong> Mentions and backlinks from credible third-party publishers act as authority signals for AI systems. LinkedIn, Reddit, and industry publications are among the most-cited domains across AI platforms.</li> 
 <li><strong>Answer specific, multi-word queries.</strong> Answer engine users phrase queries conversationally and at length — average AI query length is 23 words versus 4 words for traditional search. Optimize for those long-form questions explicitly.</li> 
 <li><strong>Keep information consistent across properties.</strong> AI models skip citing brands with conflicting data across their website, LinkedIn, review sites, and Wikipedia. Audit entity information for consistency.</li> 
 <li><strong>Target bottom-funnel queries.</strong> According to <a href="https://www.position.digital/blog/ai-seo-statistics/">Position Digital</a>, bottom-funnel content like case studies and pricing pages receives the highest AI referral traffic, while top-funnel “what is” content has seen the steepest drop.</li> 
</ul>

<h2>FAQs About AI Overviews and Web Traffic</h2> 
<h3>How can I tell if my pages are being used as sources in AI Overviews?</h3> 
<p>Google Search Console does not surface this natively, and other tools group things into a general “AI referral” bucket.</p> 
<p>The best approach is to manually search a brand’s top target queries in an incognito browser and note whether its site appears as a cited source in the AI Overview. Then, use a linear regression to simulate a trend into the future.</p> 
<p>For systematic tracking at scale, third-party tools like Semrush, Ahrefs, and Authoritas can monitor which of a business’s URLs appear in AI Overviews and track citation frequency over time. HubSpot AEO also helps teams monitor their presence in AI-generated results and uncover which pages are earning visibility, making it easier to prioritize optimization efforts.</p> 
<h3>Do AI Overviews affect branded and non-branded traffic differently?</h3> 
<p>Yes, significantly. Non-branded informational queries are where AI Overviews most commonly appear and where CTR losses are steepest. Branded traffic tends to be more resilient because navigational and branded queries trigger AI Overviews at a lower rate.</p> 
<p>Try using <a href="https://developers.google.com/search/blog/2025/11/search-console-branded-filter">Google Search Console’s new branded/non-branded filter</a> to track both segments independently.</p> 
<h3>Should I change my keyword strategy because of AI Overviews?</h3> 
<p>Partially, but don’t abandon informational content entirely. Factual, educational content is still valuable for building topical authority and earning AI citations. But marketers should rebalance their investment toward comparison content, bottom-funnel queries, and original research that AI can’t fully synthesize.</p> 
<p>The goal is to be the source AI cites, not to avoid the queries AI covers. Shift success metrics from pure click volume to share of voice, citation frequency, and branded search growth.</p> 
<h3>When should you shift budget toward owned channels?</h3> 
<p>At the risk of sounding dramatic: <em>now</em>. If more than 50% of a business’s web traffic currently comes from non-branded organic search, it is overexposed.</p> 
<p>Email lists, communities, newsletters, and direct audience relationships are immune to AI Overview cannibalization, algorithm updates, or shifts in Google’s rendering. The value of owning an audience compounds over time; it’s the one distribution channel where a business’s results are entirely its own.</p> 
<p>Publishers with high branded and direct traffic, like the Daily Mail (whose <a href="https://digiday.com/media/daily-mail-says-google-ai-overviews-have-killed-click-throughs/">over 60% of traffic</a> is direct) have proven significantly more resilient to AI Overview disruption than sites reliant on non-branded organic search.</p>

<h2>Website traffic is reincarnating.</h2> 
<p>AI is not killing web traffic — it’s redistributing it. Clicks are declining for informational queries, especially non-branded ones. But traffic from AI citations, for the brands that earn it, converts at rates that dwarf traditional organic search.</p> 
<p>The marketers who win in the battle against AI impact on website traffic are the ones who stop measuring success purely in clicks and start experimenting with measuring visibility, citation frequency, and audience ownership. The structural change is real, and it isn’t reversing. What changes is whether you’re on the right side of it.</p>
