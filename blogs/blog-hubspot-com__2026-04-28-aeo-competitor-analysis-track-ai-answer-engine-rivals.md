<h2><strong>What is AEO competitor analysis?</strong></h2> 
<p>AEO competitor analysis is the process of identifying which brands, pages, and sources answer engines cite in AI-generated responses — and benchmarking a brand’s own visibility against competitors across those same queries.</p> 
<p>“AEO” stands for <a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/answer-engine-optimization">Answer Engine Optimization</a>: the practice of structuring content so that AI platforms like ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google’s AI Overviews, and Gemini surface it as a trusted answer.</p> 
<p>AEO competitor analysis extends that practice outward — instead of marketing teams just optimizing their own content, they’re systematically tracking <em>who else</em> the engines are citing, <em>why</em>, and <em>what gaps they can close</em>.</p> 
<p>I’ve found that teams often confuse AEO with <a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/seo-competitor-analysis">traditional SEO competitive research</a>. The key difference: Traditional SEO competitor analysis tracks keyword rankings and backlinks. AEO competitor analysis tracks <strong>citation frequency</strong>, <strong>answer share</strong>, <strong>entity coverage</strong>, and <strong>QA content depth</strong> across AI-generated answers. The units of measurement are different because the underlying competition is different — marketers and SEO leaders are not fighting for a rank position, they’re fighting to be the source an LLM trusts.</p> 
<p><a href="https://www.hubspot.com/products/aeo">HubSpot AEO</a> helps marketers track how their brand appears across answer engines, showing which prompts cite competitors instead and where they’re completely absent, so teams can benchmark visibility against rivals in a single view.</p>

<h2><strong>Why AEO Competitor Analysis Matters Now</strong></h2> 
<h3><strong>Emerging channels may favor front-running adopters.</strong></h3> 
<p><a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/adapting-to-the-new-era-of-search">Answer engine search</a> is not a future trend, so stop thinking that way. It’s a current channel with accelerating adoption. According to <a href="https://searchengineland.com/google-search-zero-click-study-2024-443869">Search Engine Land</a>, 58.5% of U.S. Google searches and 59.7% of EU searches result in zero clicks. Meanwhile, ChatGPT has surpassed <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/27/chatgpt-reaches-900m-weekly-active-users/">900 million weekly active users</a>.</p> 
<p>Teams that build AEO measurement and content infrastructure now are establishing citation authority before most competitors have even started tracking it.</p> 
<p>I’ve spoken with SEO leaders who treat AI visibility as a “wait and see” channel. My experience has taught me that’s a mistake. Citation patterns in LLMs tend to be sticky — once a model associates a brand with authority on a topic, that association persists across queries and model updates.</p> 
<h3><strong>AI answers compress traditional SERPs.</strong></h3> 
<p>Google’s AI Overviews push organic blue links further down the page, often below the fold. For high-intent queries — “what is the best CRM for startups,” “how do I calculate customer lifetime value” — the AI answer <em>is</em> the SERP result for most users. If a competitor is consistently cited in those answers and a brand is not, that brand is effectively invisible for those queries, regardless of its rankings.</p> 
<h3><strong>Visibility shifts to citations, entities, and QA patterns.</strong></h3> 
<p>Traditional search rewards pages. Answer engines reward <em>entities</em> and <em>answers</em>. Answer engines evaluate content based on:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li><strong>Citation frequency.</strong> How often is a brand or URL cited for a given topic set?</li> 
 <li><strong>Entity coverage.</strong> Does the content clearly establish what the brand is, what it does, and who it serves?</li> 
 <li><strong>QA depth.</strong> Does the content directly and completely answer the questions users are actually asking?</li> 
</ul> 
<p>Competitor analysis in this environment means understanding not just what a brand’s rivals are publishing, but <em>how their content is structured</em> and <em>why LLMs prefer it</em>.</p> 
<p>HubSpot AEO breaks down which domains, content types, and sources answer engines are citing most often, giving marketers clear insight into what content is currently favored and what they need to create or optimize to improve visibility.</p> 
<h3><strong>Impact on pipeline influence, support deflection, and brand authority.</strong></h3> 
<p>AEO visibility has a downstream business impact beyond traffic. Brands that appear consistently in AI answers for buying-stage queries — “best [category] software,” “how to choose a [tool],” “[brand A] vs [brand B]” — influence purchase decisions before a prospect ever visits a website.</p> 
<p>Teams tracking AEO competitor data are also using it to identify support and product FAQ opportunities, deflecting inbound questions by owning AI-generated answers to common customer issues.</p> 
<p>HubSpot AEO and <a href="https://www.hubspot.com/products/marketing/aeo">AEO features in Marketing Hub Pro and Enterprise</a> provide a prioritized list of recommendations based on visibility and citation data, helping teams turn competitor insights into a clear plan for improving their presence in AI-generated answers.</p>

<h2><strong>How to Run an AEO Competitor Analysis Step by Step</strong></h2> 
<p><img alt="how to run an aeo competitor analysis, discover, benchmark, diagnose, act, measure" height="398" src="https://53.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hub/53/hubfs/aeo-competitor-analysis-1-20260409-3250637.webp?width=598&amp;height=398&amp;name=aeo-competitor-analysis-1-20260409-3250637.webp" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto; display: block; width: 598px; height: auto;" width="598" /></p> 
<h3><strong>Step 1: Collect priority questions that answer engines must resolve.</strong></h3> 
<p>Start by building a query set, which is a representative list of questions your target audience asks that answer engines are likely to resolve with a generated answer. These should span:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li><strong>Awareness-stage questions </strong>– “What is [category]?” / “How does [process] work?”</li> 
 <li><strong>Consideration-stage questions </strong>– “Best [tool type] for [use case]” / “[Brand A] vs [Brand B].”</li> 
 <li><strong>Decision-stage questions </strong>– “How much does [product] cost?” / “Is [brand] right for [company type]?”</li> 
 <li><strong>Support and FAQ questions </strong>– Common issues customers search for after purchase.</li> 
</ul> 
<p><strong>Pro tip:</strong> Pull questions from the existing keyword research, customer support tickets, sales call transcripts, and “People Also Ask” boxes in Google. Marketers want 30 to 100 queries across their core topic clusters to get a statistically meaningful view of answer share. For HubSpot users, built-in <a href="https://www.hubspot.com/products/marketing/aeo">AEO features</a> in Marketing Hub Pro and Enterprise suggest prompts to track based on its knowledge of their business and customers.</p> 
<h3><strong>Step 2: Test queries across chatbots and AI Overviews.</strong></h3> 
<p>Run each query manually or with an AEO tool across multiple answer engines: ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, and Gemini. Record:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li>Which sources are cited (URLs and domain names).</li> 
 <li>Which brands are mentioned by name (even without a citation link).</li> 
 <li>The structure and format of the answer (list, paragraph, table, step-by-step).</li> 
 <li>Whether your brand appears at all.</li> 
</ul> 
<p>At scale, this is where AEO tools become essential — manual testing across 50+ queries on four platforms isn’t sustainable. But I recommend starting with manual testing for a brand’s top 10 to 15 queries. It builds intuition for <em>why</em> certain content gets cited that dashboards alone won’t give you.</p> 
<p>With <a href="https://www.hubspot.com/products/aeo">HubSpot AEO</a>, marketers can automatically track prompts across ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini, seeing which responses cite their brand, which cite competitors, and how visibility changes over time without manual testing.</p> 
<h3><strong>Step 3: Extract cited sources and entities.</strong></h3> 
<p>For each query in a set, document every cited source and named entity. Marketers are building a map of:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li><strong>Which competitor domains are most frequently cited</strong> (citation frequency by domain).</li> 
 <li><strong>Which specific pages or content types win citations</strong> (blog posts, documentation, landing pages, research reports).</li> 
 <li><strong>Which entities are consistently mentioned</strong> (brand names, product names, people, organizations).</li> 
</ul> 
<p>Look for patterns. If a competitor’s blog consistently gets cited while their product pages don’t, that tells marketers something about what content format LLMs prefer. If a direct competitor is appearing for their core queries, that’s a new competitive threat worth tracking.</p> 
<h3><strong>Step 4: Map competitors by topic cluster and answer share.</strong></h3> 
<p>With citation data collected, marketers should organize it by topic cluster — not just by competitor. Calculate a rough <strong>answer share</strong> for each brand: the percentage of queries in a topic cluster where that brand is cited.</p> 
<p>This map reveals two things:</p> 
<ol start="1"> 
 <li><strong>Where competitors dominate.</strong> Topic clusters where a rival has a high answer share, and you have low or none — these are priority gap areas.</li> 
 <li><strong>Where the field is open.</strong> Topic clusters where no brand dominates — these represent fast-mover opportunities where strong content could quickly establish citation authority.</li> 
</ol>

<h3><strong>Step 5: Diagnose why competitors win.</strong></h3> 
<p>This is the step most teams skip — and it’s the most valuable. Don’t just identify <em>that</em> a competitor wins citations. Diagnose <em>why</em>.</p> 
<p>For each competitor page that consistently earns citations, analyze:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li><strong>Content format.</strong> Is it a listicle, a long-form guide, a FAQ page, a comparison article?</li> 
 <li><strong>QA structure.</strong> Does the page directly answer the question in the first 1–2 sentences, then provide supporting detail?</li> 
 <li><strong>Entity clarity.</strong> Does the page clearly state what the brand/product/topic is, who it’s for, and what problem it solves?</li> 
 <li><strong>Freshness.</strong> When was the content last updated? LLMs often favor recently updated content for fast-moving topics.</li> 
 <li><strong>Schema markup.</strong> Does the page use FAQ, HowTo, or other structured data?</li> 
 <li><strong>Backlink authority.</strong> Is the page well-cited by other authoritative sources?</li> 
</ul> 
<p><strong>What I like:</strong> The most actionable diagnostic question is: <em>“If I were a language model trying to answer this question, would this page give me a clear, trustworthy, complete answer?”</em> That framing cuts through much of the complexity.</p>

<h2><strong>AEO Competitor Analysis Metrics and Dashboards</strong></h2> 
<h3><strong>Measure answer share and citation frequency.</strong></h3> 
<p><strong>Answer share</strong> is the foundational AEO metric: the percentage of queries in a defined set where a brand is cited in the AI-generated answer. It’s the AEO equivalent of organic market share.</p> 
<p>Track answer share at three levels:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li><strong>Overall.</strong> Across a full query set.</li> 
 <li><strong>By topic cluster.</strong> To identify where a brand is winning and losing.</li> 
 <li><strong>Over time.</strong> To measure whether content investments are improving visibility.</li> 
</ul> 
<p><strong>Citation frequency</strong> is the raw count behind answer share — how many times a domain or URL is cited across the query set. High citation frequency on a small number of pages may indicate over-reliance on a few content assets; broad citation frequency across many pages signals strong topical authority.</p> 
<h3><strong>Track entity coverage and QA depth.</strong></h3> 
<p><strong>Entity coverage</strong> measures whether a brand, product, and key topics are explicitly recognized and associated correctly by answer engines. Test this by asking LLMs directly: “What is [your brand]?” / “What does [your brand] do?” / “Who uses [your product]?” If answers are vague, incomplete, or incorrect, marketers have an entity clarity problem that will suppress citations across their full query set.</p> 
<p><strong>QA depth</strong> measures how completely a brand’s content answers the specific questions in its query set. Score competitor content and your own on a simple rubric:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li>Does the page answer the question directly in the opening section?</li> 
 <li>Does it cover the question comprehensively (including follow-up questions and edge cases)?</li> 
 <li>Is the answer structured for easy extraction (headers, bullets, numbered steps)?</li> 
</ul> 
<h3><strong>Connect AI answer visibility to conversions.</strong></h3> 
<p>The hardest — and most important — AEO measurement challenge is connecting AI visibility to the pipeline. I recommend a multi-touch approach:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li><strong>UTM tracking on cited URLs.</strong> Ensure all high-priority content assets have UTM parameters so teams can track traffic from AI-referred clicks in HubSpot or GA4.</li> 
 <li><strong>Self-reported attribution.</strong> Add “How did you hear about us?” fields to forms and track “AI search” or “ChatGPT/Perplexity” as a source option. This captures influenced pipeline that never generates a tracked click.</li> 
 <li><strong>Dark social monitoring.</strong> Monitor branded search volume and direct traffic trends in parallel with AEO investments — AI answer visibility often drives brand searches that convert through direct channels.</li> 
</ul> 
<p><strong>Pro tip:</strong> In HubSpot, create a custom contact property for AI-attributed first touch. Over time, this builds a dataset that correlates AEO content investments with actual contact and deal creation.</p> 
<p>AEO in HubSpot Marketing Hub Pro and Enterprise connects AI visibility tracking to CRM data, making it possible to tie answer engine performance to contacts, pipeline, and revenue in the same reporting system.</p>

<h2><strong>Turn AEO Competitor Insights Into Actions</strong></h2> 
<p>Once your analysis is complete, translate findings into a prioritized action list. Here are the most common and highest-impact actions I’ve seen AEO competitor analysis surface:</p> 
<ul> 
 <li><strong>Create direct-answer content for high-gap queries.</strong> If a competitor earns citations on 8 out of 10 queries in a topic cluster and you earn 0, the fastest path to closing that gap is publishing purpose-built QA content that directly answers those questions — structured with a clear question as the H2, a direct answer in the first 1–2 sentences, and supporting detail below.</li> 
 <li><strong>Update and restructure existing pages.</strong> Many citation wins come from reformatting existing content rather than creating new content. Add direct answers, FAQ sections with schema markup, and clearer entity statements to pages that are already indexed and authoritative.</li> 
 <li><strong>Track AI Answers:&nbsp;</strong> <p><span>As answer engines continue to reshape how users discover brands, many of the signals that influence AI visibility still stem from strong SEO</span>&nbsp;factors, including content structure, entity clarity, and topical authority. Working with <a href="https://designpoint.com.au/seo-services/">SEO services focused on improving AI search visibility</a> can help translate AEO insights into practical actions, ensuring your content is not only indexed but also cited and trusted across AI-generated overview answers.</p> </li> 
 <li><strong>Build entity disambiguation content.</strong> If LLMs give incomplete or inaccurate answers about your brand, publish an authoritative “About” or “What is [Brand]?” page with structured entity information. Reinforce entity signals across your site and in third-party sources (Wikipedia, Crunchbase, press coverage).</li> 
 <li><strong>Prioritize topic clusters where answer share is low but competitor content is weak.</strong> Not every gap requires competing with a dominant rival. Look for clusters where no competitor has strong AEO content — those are the fastest paths to establishing first-mover citation authority.</li> 
 <li><strong>Add comparison and “best for” content.</strong> Comparison queries (“X vs. Y,” “best [tool] for [use case]”) are high-intent and frequently answered by LLMs. If competitors are winning these queries and you’re not, comparison content is a high-priority gap to close.</li> 
 <li><strong>Strengthen internal linking between high-performing and low-performing pages.</strong> LLMs index topical authority signals across a domain. Pages that aren’t earning citations may benefit from stronger connections to your most-cited content.</li> 
 <li><strong>Submit updated content to Google for re-indexing.</strong> For pages you’ve updated to improve QA depth or entity clarity, use Google Search Console to request re-indexing so updated signals are picked up quickly.</li> 
 <li><strong>Track changes at monthly intervals.</strong> AEO competitive dynamics shift as competitors publish new content and as LLMs update. Build a monthly cadence of running your priority query set and updating your answer share benchmarks.</li> 
</ul>

<h2><strong>Frequently Asked Questions About AEO Competitor Analysis</strong></h2> 
<h3><strong>How often should you run AEO competitor analysis?</strong></h3> 
<p>I recommend a full AEO competitor analysis — running your complete query set, documenting citations, and updating benchmarks — on a <strong>monthly cadence</strong> for most teams.</p> 
<p>For competitive markets or during active content campaigns, biweekly monitoring of top-priority query clusters is worth the investment. Unlike traditional SEO rankings, which update continuously, AI citation patterns can shift meaningfully after a competitor publishes new content or after a model update — so regular snapshots are necessary to detect changes.</p> 
<h3><strong>How do you attribute pipeline impact from AI answers?</strong></h3> 
<p>Pipeline attribution for AI answers requires a combination of methods because AI-generated answers don’t always generate trackable clicks.</p> 
<p>Use UTM-tagged URLs on cited content to capture direct referral traffic, add answer engines as a self-reported attribution option on forms and in sales conversations, and monitor branded search and direct traffic trends as a proxy for AI-influenced awareness.</p> 
<p>In HubSpot, custom contact properties and deal source fields let you build a longitudinal view of an AI-attributed pipeline over time. Within HubSpot Marketing Hub, marketers can use CRM data, custom properties, and reporting tools to track AI-influenced contacts and build a clearer view of how AEO contributes to pipeline over time.</p> 
<h3><strong>What is the best way to structure QA content for LLM citations?</strong></h3> 
<p>The content format most consistently cited by LLMs is the <strong>direct-answer structure</strong>: the target question appears verbatim (or near-verbatim) as an H2 or H3 heading; the first 1–3 sentences provide a complete, direct answer to that question; supporting detail, examples, and nuance follow in clearly organized subsections.</p> 
<p>FAQ schema markup reinforces this structure for Google’s AI Overviews. HowTo schema works similarly for process-oriented content. Avoid burying the answer in lengthy preambles — LLMs favor content that gets to the point immediately.</p> 
<h3><strong>When should you prioritize AEO over traditional SEO?</strong></h3> 
<p>AEO and traditional SEO are not mutually exclusive — the same content quality signals that drive rankings (authority, depth, structured formatting, freshness) also drive AI citations.</p> 
<p>However, if analytics show declining organic click-through rates despite stable or improving rankings, that’s a signal that AI answers are intercepting clicks for your target queries. In that scenario, investing in AEO content structure and citation optimization is likely to have a higher marginal return than chasing additional ranking improvements.</p> 
<p>More broadly, for any query type where AI Overviews or LLM answers are already dominant, AEO should be the primary optimization lens.</p>

<h2><strong>From Analysis to Action: Turning AEO Insights Into Competitive Advantage</strong></h2> 
<p>AEO competitor analysis gives marketers something traditional SEO never fully could: a direct view into how brands are actually recommended at the moment of decision-making. Instead of optimizing for rankings alone, teams can now measure citation frequency, answer share, and entity presence — and understand exactly why competitors are being surfaced in AI-generated answers.</p> 
<p>The real value, however, comes from what happens next. Identifying gaps is only useful if teams can act on them quickly and consistently. That’s where tools like HubSpot’s AEO Grader provide an accessible starting point, helping marketers benchmark their current visibility and understand how they compare. From there, HubSpot AEO and AEO features in HubSpot Marketing Hub enable ongoing tracking, competitor analysis, and prioritized recommendations — while also connecting those insights directly to content execution, CRM data, and pipeline reporting.</p> 
<p>For teams investing in AEO, the path forward is clear: Build a reliable query set, track answer share over time, and continuously refine content based on what AI engines actually cite. The companies that operationalize this process early won’t just keep up with competitors — they’ll define how their category is represented in answer engines.</p>
